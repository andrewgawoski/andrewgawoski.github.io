Luna Limit (2016)

About the Project

Luna Limit is a single player tile-based game with a strong simple math influence. Players activate tiles to change surrounding moon phases in a way similar to adding simple numbers. Tasked with a single objective, players attempt to create as many full moons as possible.

Features

  • Combine different moon phases
  • Create full and new moons
  • Rack up score multipliers
  • Large number of different game boards

View Source

With the goal of creating a simple and intuitive game that would encourage more casual pick-up and play mentality, Luna Limit focused nearly entirely on moon phases and how intuitive mechanics based on them would be. Early on, this was prototyped using numbers ranging between -6 and 7, allowing for continuous addition of numbers with the player goal of reaching certain numbers: 7 and to a lesser extent 0. Functionally, mechanically, everything worked. As a solo dev, I was using the project to refamiliarize myself with Unity’s workflow and I was very successful in that sense. I took the opportunity to organize my tasks in an agile manner similar to scrum, something easily reusable for future projects. Asset creation was simple and completed early on in the development process.

However, the assets themselves created the issue leading to Luna Limit’s end and cancellation. Despite creating the mechanic myself, once I implemented the lunar phase sprites for the tile prefabs, I discovered just how unintuitive adding phases of the moon could be. While adding small slivers of white together to create larger slivers makes a lot of sense, it was not intuitive when the phases of the moon rolled over to progress to a new moon and white and black were added together to create even larger quantities of black. Branching the game to a state where numbers existed instead of moon sprites, I confirmed the source of the difficulty and was left with a killer problem. If the number value of the tiles cannot progress past 7 to the point where you progress towards a new moon, there is no longer enough challenge in the game to hold interest. If things stay as they are, the game is too confusing to be played effectively. If the moon sprites are removed, all that remains is a fairly plain game with numbered tiles that doesn’t have any other appeal.

It’s better to look at Luna Limit as an experiment looking to discover if moon-phase addition was an intuitive mechanic. At least in this implementation, I found the driving mechanic for this game’s creation to be unintuitive, and have ceased work on it. For the purpose of learning, sharing and other forms of curiosity, I’ve provided the game as is under MIT license.
